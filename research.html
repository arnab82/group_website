Research Interests

Critical problems in all areas of chemistry benefit from a microscopic understanding of structure and mechanism. Computational chemistry provides an exceptionally detail-rich glance into the inner workings of molecular events. Our research activities focus primarily on the development of novel quantum chemistry methods and the application of these methods to investigate the chemical foundations of renewable energy sources.

Excited states in molecular materials

By incorporating photoactive molecules into a Metal Organic Framework (MOF), highly regular and long-range chromophoric arrays can be synthesized to yield new materials displaying energy harvesting capabilities, with potential for use in next generation energy solutions. We develop computational techniques to understand the detailed mechanisms which characterize such properties, in an effort to design new materials with tailored photophysics.

Strong correlation in molecules

Entangled electrons in molecular magnets, strongly correlated transition metal catalysts, many of our most important problems are not easily modelled with conventional quantum chemistry. We have an on-going interest in developing new tools for the computational chemist to efficiently model systems containing strongly correlated electrons.

Efficient calculations of large molecules

Accurate quantum chemistry is difficult to apply to large molecules because the calculations quickly become too computationally demanding. Approaches to solve this problem can often be divided into two categories: 1) Local orbital methods: More rigorous, but complicated and often slow to crossover. Can be very accurate. 2) Fragment-based methods: Simple approach, well suited for large distributed computing environments. Difficulties with accuracy.
